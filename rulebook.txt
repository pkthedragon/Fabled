Fabled — Official Technical Game Manual
FABLED
A Guildmaster's Game
Official Rulebook and Systems Reference

You just inherited a guild in the kingdom of Fantasia, home of the mystical and magical. Your house is short on prestige, short on gold, and rich only in possibility. Adventurers who cannot find work elsewhere still pass through your doors, and your role as Guildmaster is to turn that castoff talent into a disciplined force capable of conquering Quests, winning Bouts, and restoring your family's standing.
Luckily, guildcraft in Fabled is not only about recruiting strong heroes. It is about understanding timing, positioning, attrition, scouting, adaptation, and how dozens of interacting passives and spells reshape each round. This rulebook is the complete technical reference for those systems while preserving the appendix structure the game uses for live reference data.

1. OVERVIEW
Fabled is a tactical turn-based combat and guild-management game focused on positioning, ability timing, target legality, and loadout mastery. The player manages a Guild Hall, builds six-member parties, configures weapons, classes, class skills, and artifacts, and then commits three-member formations into individual encounters.
The game is built around several overlapping loops. The encounter loop is the battle itself: choose actions, resolve initiative, manage conditions, and win through party elimination or Ultimate Victory. The run loop belongs to Quests, Bouts, and Tutorial play: scout opponents, choose which three to deploy, adapt loadouts, and manage resources between battles. The guild loop handles persistent Gold, Reputation, Guild Exp, employee configuration, cosmetics, social data, and first-run onboarding.
The major playable destinations are Tutorial Mode, Quests, Random Bouts, Focused Bouts, Training Grounds, the Codex, the Market, the Closet, and the Guild Hall overlays for profile, friends, LAN, settings, and employee configuration.
This manual is organized as follows. Section 2 defines the core game entities. Section 3 explains how encounters resolve, including formation, timing, actions, conditions, and the Ultimate Meter. Sections 4 through 9 cover Quests, Bouts, progression, the Guild Hall, the Market, and Tutorial Mode. Sections 10 through 14 cover reference surfaces, social systems, interface expectations, technical reference notes, and glossary terminology.

2. CORE CONCEPTS
2.1 Adventurers
Adventurers are the primary controllable combatants in Fabled. Each adventurer has fixed identity data and configurable loadout data.
Fixed identity data:
Name and presentation identity
Base HP, Attack, Defense, and Speed
Innate Skill
Two Signature Weapons
Ultimate Spell
Configurable loadout data:
Primary Weapon choice
Class assignment
Chosen Class Skill
Optional Artifact
Encounter formation assignment when selected into the active three

2.2 Quest Enemies
Quest enemies are not pulled from the player's adventurer roster. They are authored hostile combatants tied to locales and tiers. Unlike player adventurers, quest enemies may use simplified kits, enemy-only skills, enemy-only artifacts, or Apex dual-primary setups depending on tier. Humanoid enemies can use classes and class skills, while nonhumanoid enemies rely more heavily on innate kits.

2.3 Skills, Spells, Ultimates, and Reactions
Fabled distinguishes several types of gameplay effects:
Skills are passive effects that remain active while their source is active.
Spells are activated effects chosen during an action or bonus-action window and usually placed on cooldown after use.
Ultimate Spells are special action choices that require a full Ultimate Meter.
Reactive Spells are not manually selected; they trigger automatically when their condition is met.
There is no global hard cap on how many skills or spells a combatant can have, but only effects from active sources matter. For example, a normal adventurer only benefits from the current primary weapon's passives and spells.

2.4 Weapons
Weapons define Strike behavior and may add passive skills or spells. Every weapon has a type and a strike definition, and some also have ammo or cooldown constraints.
Melee. May only Strike from the frontline and normally only targets the enemy frontline.
Ranged. May Strike from any position, targets the enemy frontline or a same-lane backline enemy, and consumes Ammo unless prevented.
Magic. Uses Ranged legality, but Magic Strikes count as spellcasting and usually place the weapon on cooldown after use.
Every normal adventurer owns two Signature Weapons. Before or between encounters, the player assigns one as primary and the other as secondary. Only the primary weapon's skills and spells are active during combat unless a specific rule says otherwise. Apex enemies are the major exception: they wield two simultaneous primaries.

2.5 Classes
There are six standard classes:
Warden, the tanking and positional control class
Fighter, the primary Melee damage class
Mage, the primary Magic damage class
Ranger, the primary Ranged damage class
Cleric, the healing and support class
Rogue, the disruption and speed class
Each class grants access to two class skills, of which exactly one is selected. Class skills always grant bonus HP and usually define a major playstyle fork. A six-member party may contain at most one adventurer of each class. Appendix B contains the full class skill list, including enemy-only legendary skills.

2.6 Artifacts
Artifacts are optional equipment that provide a stat bonus and at least one spell. Standard player-facing artifacts typically provide one spell. Enemy legendary artifacts can provide both an activated spell and a reactive spell.
Artifact rules:
Only one copy of any specific artifact may be equipped across the party at one time.
Each artifact lists attunement classes. A holder always gains the artifact's stat bonus, but may only use its spell if their current class matches one of the attunement classes unless the artifact is explicitly authored otherwise.
The artifact slot is optional.
Reactive Spells are not actions. They trigger automatically when their condition is satisfied and then go on cooldown.
Artifacts that are not equipped live in the current run's Artifact Pool.
Appendix C lists standard artifacts. Appendix D lists enemy artifacts.

2.7 Parties, Benches, and Pools
Most non-tutorial run formats are built around a six-member party. Only three members are active in any individual encounter. The other three remain on the bench and keep their loadouts unless changed in a legal editing step.
Key terms:
Party means the full six-member run roster.
Bench means the three or fewer members not currently deployed.
Artifact Pool means unequipped artifacts available within the active Quest or Bout.
Active three means the currently deployed encounter team.

3. ENCOUNTERS
An encounter is a single battle between two teams of up to three active combatants each. Most combat surfaces in Fabled, including Quests, Bouts, Tutorial fights, and practice encounters, all run on the same core encounter engine.
3.1 Formation
Each side occupies a triangle formation with one Frontline slot and two Backline slots, Left and Right. The Frontline slots oppose each other directly and the Backline slots create left and right lanes behind them.
Key formation rules:
Exactly one unit must occupy the frontline at all times while any unit remains.
Up to two units may occupy the backline.
Backline units have their Speed halved for initiative purposes.
If the frontline unit is knocked out, the leftmost surviving backline unit automatically advances; if Backline Left is empty, Backline Right advances instead.
Position matters not only for defense but also for legality, speed, and how certain passives trigger.

3.2 Lanes and Targeting Legality
The formation creates three lanes:
center lane, occupied by the frontlines
left lane, shared by Backline Left on each side
right lane, shared by Backline Right on each side
Melee effects generally require the user to be in the frontline and normally target the enemy frontline only.
Ranged and Magic effects can target the enemy frontline or a same-lane backline target.
Spotlight modifies legality by allowing Melee access to a backline target.
Effects that say they ignore targeting restrictions temporarily override the normal legality map.

3.3 Stats and Effective State
Every combatant ultimately resolves actions using effective stats, not printed base stats. Effective stats combine:
base stats
class-skill HP bonus
artifact stat bonus
the strongest active positive stat modifier
the strongest active negative stat modifier
current positional penalty or benefit
The game does not ordinarily stack multiple positive bonuses to the same stat or multiple negative penalties to the same stat. It uses the single highest positive and single highest negative effect currently active.

3.4 Duration Semantics
If an effect lasts for 1 round, 2 rounds, or more, it lasts that many full rounds after the round in which it was applied.
This means:
1 round lasts for the remainder of the current round plus the next full round.
2 rounds lasts for the remainder of the current round plus the next two full rounds.
Separate from that, some effects last only for this round, until the next Strike, until the next Spell, or once per encounter. Those effects are consumed by trigger rather than ordinary round countdown.

3.5 Round Structure
Every round proceeds in this order:
Initiative Check
Start of Round Effects
Action Selection
Action Resolution
Bonus Action Selection
Bonus Action Resolution
End of Round Effects

Initiative is checked once at round start using current effective Speed. Tie-breakers are:
lower Power active primary weapon first
higher party average Speed first
coinflip if still tied
Because initiative is locked for the round, later Speed changes do not retroactively reorder already-planned actions in that same round.

3.6 Action Selection and Resolution
Both sides choose actions before anything resolves. Actions then resolve in initiative order, which means a target that was legal during selection can become illegal before a slower unit acts. A later action may fail because:
its target moved
its target was knocked out
frontline replacement changed lane occupancy
an immunity, reactive effect, or legality override invalidated the original plan

3.7 Core Actions
Strike
Uses the active weapon's Strike definition.
Base damage formula:
Damage = ceil(Power × (Attack / Defense))
Flat power bonuses or reductions apply before the Attack-to-Defense scaling. Percentage damage dealt or damage taken modifiers apply after scaling unless an effect states otherwise. Caps, floor rules, and replacement effects apply after rounding as needed.

Spellcast
Uses one available spell from the combatant's active sources. Enemy-targeting spells usually follow lane legality; ally-targeting spells may usually target any ally. Spells normally enter cooldown after use.

Switch Weapons
Swaps primary and secondary weapons, resets cooldowns on both, and fully reloads ranged ammo. It also changes which weapon passives and weapon spells are active. Switch is illegal for units without two switchable weapons and for Apex enemies, who already wield both primaries.

Swap Positions
Swaps the acting unit with a target ally. If the target has not acted yet, they resolve from their new position. Each side may usually choose the Swap action for only one unit per round, although abilities may still cause extra movement.

Skip
Consumes the action and does nothing directly. Some passives still care whether the unit skipped, switched, or swapped.

Ultimate
Available only when the shared Ultimate Meter is full. Consumes the action and empties the meter.

3.8 Conditions
Conditions are temporary status effects. Different conditions stack together. The same condition does not stack with itself; reapplying it refreshes duration instead.

Burn
Deals 8% of max HP at end of round.
Root
Prevents the unit from using Swap Positions.
Shock
Applies -25 Speed and causes the unit's Strikes to deal 15% recoil to itself.
Weaken
Causes the unit to deal 15% less damage.
Expose
Causes the unit to take 15% more damage.
Guard
Causes the unit to take 15% less damage.
Spotlight
Allows the unit to be targeted by Melee while in the backline.
Taunt
Restricts the unit so it may only target the Taunt source unless that target is illegal.

3.9 Special Keywords
Spread causes an effect to hit all legal targets, usually at half damage each unless a modifier removes the penalty.
Lifesteal restores HP based on damage actually dealt.
Recoil deals self-damage based on damage dealt.
Ammo gates repeated use of some Ranged Strikes.
Cooldown prevents repeated spell or Magic-Strike usage until enough rounds pass.

3.10 The Ultimate Meter
Each side has a shared Ultimate Meter starting at 0.
Non-Magic Strike: +1 meter
Non-Ultimate Spell cast: +2 meter
Meter cap: 5
Ultimate cast: meter resets to 0
Starting from round 7, the meter auto-fills by +1 at end of round.
Starting from round 12, it auto-fills by +2 instead.

3.11 Ending an Encounter
An encounter ends immediately when either win condition is satisfied:
Party Elimination. One side has no surviving active combatants.
Ultimate Victory. One side casts its third Ultimate Spell during the encounter.

4. QUESTS
Quests are the primary single-player progression mode. A Quest is a live run with its own party, artifact pool, gold total, reputation delta, streak state, and loss count. The run continues until the player reaches three losses or chooses to forfeit.

4.1 Starting a Quest
The standard Quest start sequence is:
Choose a Favorite. The player selects one previously quested-with adventurer who is guaranteed a place in the six-member party.
Receive five more adventurers. The random generation is constrained so that the party can plausibly cover classes rather than collapsing into unusable role overlap.
Receive starting artifacts. Three random artifacts are added to the run's Artifact Pool with attunement-diversity constraints.
Set full-party loadouts. The player chooses each member's primary weapon, class, class skill, and any equipped artifact from the pool.

4.2 Quest Run State
A Quest tracks:
the six-member party
the current deployed trio per encounter
the current Artifact Pool
unspent Quest gold
running Quest reputation total
current winstreak and lossstreak for matchmaking and payouts
current loss count
encounters won

These values are run-specific. They are only converted into permanent progression at the end of the Quest.

4.3 Encounter Flow Inside a Quest
Each Quest encounter proceeds through:
Scout. The player sees the enemy trio and associated context such as tier, locale, or authored title where revealed.
Select and Form. The player chooses which three party members to bring and arranges their formation.
Encounter-only adjustments. During this step the player may swap artifacts between party members where the flow allows and change class skills for the selected combatants, but may not freely rebuild the entire six-member party from scratch.
Fight. The encounter resolves under the combat rules in Section 3.
Results. Reputation and gold are updated, and rewards or penalties are applied.
Regroup. The player returns to the full loadout editor and may freely reconfigure the party for the next scout.

4.4 Winning a Quest Encounter
On a win, the player gains reputation for the Quest's running total and then chooses a reward.
Reputation gain formula:
ReputationGain = round(BaseChange + WinstreakBonus), clamped from 8 to 30
BaseChange = 28 × (ActualScore - ExpectedScore)
ActualScore = 1
ExpectedScore = 1 / (1 + 10^((OpponentReputation - PlayerReputation) / 400))
WinstreakBonus = min(current Quest winstreak before this win, 5) × 2

Reward options:
Gold. Gain 100 Gold in the Quest gold pool.
Artifact. Choose one of three offered artifacts and add it to the Quest Artifact Pool.
Recruit. Spend 100 Gold to replace one party member with one of three offered adventurers. The departing member's equipped artifact, if any, returns to the pool.

4.5 Losing a Quest Encounter
On a loss:
-10 Reputation is applied to the Quest's running reputation total.
-50 Gold is removed from the Quest gold pool, to a minimum of 0.
The loss count increases by one.
When the third loss is recorded, the Quest ends immediately after that encounter's resolution flow.

4.6 Successful Quest Definition
A Quest is successful if its final net reputation total is positive. This matters for employee effects and for how the run is interpreted by some reward systems.

4.7 End of Quest
At Quest end, resolution happens in this order:
Sell Artifacts. All artifacts still in the run, equipped or pooled, are sold for 100 Gold each unless another system modifies that value.
Collect Gold. The resulting Quest gold total is added to permanent Gold, subject to Assistant skill modifiers.
Apply Reputation. The Quest's net reputation change is added to permanent Reputation.
Award Guild Exp. Exp is granted based on encounters won.

4.8 Forfeiting a Quest
Forfeit ends the run immediately and applies:
Forfeit Penalty = -10 Reputation × losses remaining
Losses remaining equals 3 minus the current loss count. Standard end-of-Quest resolution then occurs.

4.9 Reputation and Ranks
Reputation is the persistent ranked measure primarily earned through Quests.
Squire: 1-199
Knight: 200-399
Baron: 400-599
Viscount: 600-799
Earl: 800-999
Margrave: 1,000-1,199
Duke: 1,200-1,399
Prince: 1,400-1,599
King: 1,600-1,799
Emperor: 1,800-2,000

4.10 Match Rating
Quest enemy strength scales using Match Rating:
Match Rating = Reputation + (20 × current winstreak) - (15 × current lossstreak)
This nudges hot runs toward tougher encounters and gives slightly softer matchmaking after consecutive losses.

4.11 Quest Enemies
Quest enemies are creatures, monsters, spirits, rivals, and named antagonists from Fantasia rather than player-owned adventurers. They obey the combat engine but are built from authored enemy definitions.
Humanoid enemies use classes and class skills where their tier requires them.
Nonhumanoid enemies rely on innate kits when a comparable humanoid would instead use a class skill.
Enemies do not ordinarily have Ultimate Spells unless an enemy is specifically authored with one.

4.12 Enemy Tiers
Tier 1
Primary weapon with Strike only, and either a class skill or an innate skill.

Tier 2
Full primary weapon, and either a class skill or an innate skill.

Tier 3
Full primary weapon, class skill, and innate skill.

Tier 4
Full primary weapon, full secondary weapon, class skill, innate skill, and an artifact. These are unique named enemies.

Tier 5
Full primary weapon, full secondary weapon, class skill, innate skill, and a legendary artifact. These are unique named enemies.

Apex
Two simultaneous primary weapons, a legendary class skill, an innate skill, and a legendary artifact. Apex enemies can choose between two distinct Strike profiles each round, and the skills and spells from both weapons are active.

Tier 4 through Apex enemies are unique mythic or fairytale figures. Legendary artifacts are enemy-specific pieces that grant +50 to one stat and include both an activated spell and a reactive spell. Legendary class skills are Apex-only and are listed in Appendix B.

4.13 Locales
Each quest encounter takes place in one of six locales, and only enemies native to that locale may appear there.
The Forest of Dreams. A vivid magical forest full of wonder and beasts.
The Cloud Peaks. Floating mountain ranges reached by leaping across drifting rocks.
The Blackwells. Vast bog-seas and swamps inhabited by vile creatures.
The Static Plains. Yellow plains, storm flora, and mile-high lightning-struck trees.
The Scar. A deep world-crack leading toward the underworld.
The High Court. The kingdom and surrounding civilized territories of Fantasia.

Each locale contains 6 Tier 1 enemies, 4 Tier 2 enemies, 4 Tier 3 enemies, 3 Tier 4 enemies, 3 Tier 5 enemies, and 1 Apex enemy.

5. BOUTS
Bouts are Fabled's competitive set-play mode. A Bout is a best-of-three series, and the first side to win two encounters wins the set. Bouts can be played against AI or over LAN. Unlike Quests, Bout enemy sides are always built from adventurers rather than authored quest-enemy rosters.

5.1 Shared Bout Structure
Every Bout encounter follows the same broad pattern:
Scout the opposing six-member side
Choose which three to bring
Assign formation
Fight the encounter
Adapt or adjust before the next encounter if the set is not over

The combat engine is the same as in Quests. The differences lie in how parties are assembled and what can change between encounters.

5.2 Random Bouts
Random Bouts mirror Quest-style party acquisition.
Setup:
Each side selects a favorite adventurer and receives five additional random adventurers.
Each side receives a starting Artifact Pool of three random artifacts using the same diversity logic used by Quests.
Each side sets full-party loadouts before the first encounter.

Between encounters, if the set is still active, each side receives one free adaptation package:
Add Artifact. Choose one from a three-artifact offer and add it to the pool.
Replace Adventurer. Replace one party member with one of three offered adventurers. Any artifact on the departing member returns to the pool.

After adaptation, full legal loadout editing is available before the next encounter.

5.3 Random Bout Stakes
Against another player, the Bartender determines the entry fee and payout structure. Each player pays their own fee and receives their own payout on victory. Random Bouts do not change Reputation or Rank.
Against AI, Random Bouts have no entry fee and no Gold reward, but still grant Guild Exp.

5.4 Focused Bouts
Focused Bouts are the constructed format.
Each side manually builds a six-member party from the full adventurer roster.
Each side manually chooses equipped artifacts and may place up to three additional artifacts into the pool.
Each side assigns full loadouts before the set begins.

After each encounter, if the set is still active, the player may adjust loadouts using only the existing roster and pool. No new adventurers or artifacts are introduced mid-set.

5.5 Focused Bout Stakes
Focused Bouts are a pure competition and practice format.
No entry fee
No Gold reward
No Reputation change
Guild Exp awarded on win or loss

6. PROGRESSION
Fabled tracks three major persistent progression currencies: Reputation, Gold, and Guild Exp.

6.1 Reputation
Reputation is the game's ranked competitive measure and is earned primarily through Quests. It drives rank titles and is also used inside Quest matchmaking to scale encounter difficulty.

6.2 Gold
Gold is the persistent economy currency. It is earned from Quest payouts, certain Bout outcomes, level-up rewards, duplicate-cosmetic conversions, and some employee bonuses. Gold is primarily spent in the Market, though some run-time decisions such as Quest recruiting also consume run-local gold before that value is paid out permanently.

6.3 Guild Exp
Guild Exp is the account-level progression currency tied to Guild Level.
Quest Exp = 50 + (20 × encounters won during the Quest)
Random Bout Exp: 80 on win, 30 on loss
Focused Bout Exp: 50 on win, 20 on loss
Random Bouts pay more Exp because they can involve higher stakes or adaptation pressure. Focused Bouts still contribute to progression even though they do not pay Gold or Reputation.

6.4 Guild Level Curve
The Guild starts at Level 1.
Exp to next level = current level × 100
Excess Exp carries over after each level-up.

Representative cumulative thresholds:
Level 2: 100 total Exp
Level 5: 1,000 total Exp
Level 10: 4,500 total Exp
Level 15: 10,500 total Exp
Level 20: 19,000 total Exp
Level 25: 30,000 total Exp
Level 30: 43,500 total Exp
Level 40: 78,000 total Exp
Level 50: 122,500 total Exp

Guild Level primarily gates Market inventory and broader account progression.

6.5 Level-Up Rewards
Every level-up grants Gold:
Level-Up Gold = 50 + (10 × new level)

Examples:
Level 2 grants 70 Gold
Level 10 grants 150 Gold
Level 25 grants 300 Gold

Some employee skills modify this payout.

6.6 Cosmetic Milestone Rewards
Every 5 levels, the player receives one random Common cosmetic.
Every 10 levels, the player receives one random Rare cosmetic in addition to the Common reward.
For level-up reward purposes:
Common cosmetic value = 650 Gold
Rare cosmetic value = 2,000 Gold

Duplicate protection applies. If the player already owns the entire reward pool for the awarded rarity, the duplicate is auto-sold for its gold value instead.

7. THE GUILD HALL
The Guild Hall is the player's persistent home base and primary navigation hub. It provides access to Quests, Bouts, Training, Codex, Market, profile management, employee configuration, LAN play, and settings.
Employee skills can be changed between runs but are locked while a Quest or Bout is active. Their design philosophy is commitment: each skill provides a meaningful upside paired with a meaningful tradeoff.

7.1 Employees
The three core employees are:
The Assistant, who modifies non-Reputation rewards from Quests and level progression
The Bartender, who controls Random Bout entry-fee and payout structure in player-vs-player contexts
The Server, who provides a daily favorite-adventurer gold bonus

Employee appearances may be customized with owned cosmetics.

7.2 The Assistant
The Assistant never alters Reputation directly. Instead, the Assistant modifies gold and progression conversion.

Shrewd
Collect 25% more unspent Gold at end of Quest.
If the Quest was successful, the bonus becomes 50%.

Resourceful
Artifacts sell for 50% more at end of Quest.
Duplicate-cosmetic conversions also pay 50% more Gold.

Erudite
All Quest gold that would be gained is converted into Guild Exp at 1 Exp per Gold.
The player receives no Gold from that Quest.

Appraiser
Artifacts sell for 150 instead of 100 at end of Quest.
Quest Recruit costs rise from 100 to 150.

Ambitious
Level-up Gold rewards are doubled.
Unspent Gold collected from Quests is reduced by 25%.

7.3 The Bartender
The Bartender affects Random Bouts against other players. AI Random Bouts and Focused Bouts ignore Bartender economics.

Safe Income
Entry fee 100
Payout 200 on win

High Roller
Entry fee chosen between 300 and 600
Payout 2x on win
Payout 3x on a 2-0 win

Generous Pour
Entry fee 200
Payout 500 on win

Penny Pincher
Entry fee 0
Payout 100 on win

Double or Nothing
Entry fee 300
Payout 900 only on a 2-0 win
Payout 0 on a 2-1 win

7.4 The Server
The Server's Friendly effect is always active.
Each day, the Server selects a favorite adventurer from the full roster.
If the player wins a Bout or completes a successful Quest with that adventurer somewhere in the six-member party, the Server grants 100 Gold.

8. THE MARKET
The Market is the cosmetic shop. Market purchases never alter combat power, matchmaking, or encounter legality. They only change presentation.
Typical market categories and ranges:
Player Skins: 400-2,500
Player Chairs: 250-1,500
Emotes: 150-600
Poses: 150-600
Assistant Skins: 300-1,200
Bartender Skins: 300-1,200
Server Skins: 300-1,200
Adventurer Skins: 800-3,000
Guild Hall Skins: 1,500-5,000
For level-up reward purposes, Common cosmetics cost 650 Gold or less, while Rare cosmetics cost more than 650 Gold.

9. TUTORIAL MODE
Tutorial Mode is the first-run onboarding campaign. It is a ten-encounter scripted progression that teaches the game by progressively unlocking mechanics instead of showing the player the full system all at once.

9.1 First-Run Prompt
On first launch, the player is asked whether they have played Fabled before.
If the player answers no, the main menu is restricted to the tutorial entry until the tutorial is cleared.
If the player answers yes, the tutorial is skipped and the player receives the same starting gold they would have had after clearing it.

9.2 Tutorial Unlock Philosophy
Tutorial encounters progressively unlock:
Strike
Spellcast
Switch
Classes and first class skills
Swap Positions
Bonus-action awareness
Party selection and second class skills
Artifacts
Ultimates
Final full-rules combat

9.3 Tutorial Rewards
Artifacts gained during the tutorial go into a tutorial-only pool. They are usable only inside Tutorial Mode and are not converted into permanent owned combat artifacts. At tutorial completion, those tutorial artifacts are effectively sold during the graduation sequence and then cleared.

9.4 Tutorial Completion
Clearing the final tutorial encounter:
marks tutorial completion in save data
awards the game's starting permanent resources
unlocks the standard game flow
clears tutorial-only artifact ownership
marks the tutorial roster as previously quested with for later favorite selection logic

10. TRAINING, CODEX, AND REFERENCE SYSTEMS
10.1 Training Grounds
Training Grounds is the low-friction practice and inspection surface. It emphasizes roster browsing, weapon and skill readability, and quick practice fights rather than run economy.

10.2 Codex
The Codex unifies adventurer, artifact, and rules reference into a single in-game manual surface. It is informational. Seeing an artifact or adventurer entry in the Codex does not imply ownership or current-run availability.

10.3 Inspection Standard
When the UI surfaces detail on an adventurer, weapon, artifact, or enemy, the intent is that the player can understand the currently relevant gameplay information. In battle, this means the live loadout state rather than only the base biography.

11. SOCIAL AND LAN SYSTEMS
11.1 Friends
The Friends overlay stores player-managed entries containing a display name and connection IP. It is a convenience layer for LAN setup rather than a live progression system.

11.2 LAN Connect
LAN play is built around host and join flow. The LAN interface exposes the player's local IP, allows manual or friend-list IP selection, and reports connection status such as connecting, connected, or failed.

12. USER INTERFACE MODEL
12.1 Interaction Philosophy
Fabled is hover-first and drag-friendly. Information is generally revealed with popouts and tooltips rather than by leaving heavy static detail panels on screen at all times.

12.2 Core Interaction Modes
Hover reveals detail.
Click commits actions or opens overlays.
Drag and drop handles formation, artifact movement, and several loadout interactions.
Keyboard shortcuts supplement the pointer for battle speed.

12.3 Tooltip and Popout Roles
Tooltips provide short, low-density explanations for buttons, conditions, or brief labels.
Popouts provide structured detail cards for adventurers, weapons, artifacts, and live battle loadouts.

12.4 Battle Readability
The battle interface is built around:
formation-centered readability
hover inspection instead of a permanent inspect panel
inline queued-action indicators
floating combat text in place of a permanent always-open log
a toggleable overlay log for players who want the full transcript

12.5 Keyboard Shortcuts
The live battle interface supports:
1-5 for core actions
Q for Ultimate when available
TAB to cycle friendly units
SPACE to resolve when all actions are queued
ESC to cancel, back out, or close overlays

12.6 Settings
The settings overlay currently governs gameplay-presentation preferences such as Tutorial Popups, Fast Resolution, Battle Log Popups, and Screen Shake. These settings affect readability and pacing but do not alter combat legality or balance.

13. TECHNICAL REFERENCE NOTES
13.1 Authoritative Appendices
The appendices are the canonical data tables for:
all adventurers
all class skills
all standard artifacts
all enemy artifacts

13.2 Runtime Data Interpretation
The game distinguishes between printed reference data and effective runtime state. A unit's base table entry may differ from what the player sees in live battle because class bonuses, artifact bonuses, temporary buffs, penalties, or position effects have been applied.

13.3 Description Integrity
Where a description is displayed in the live UI, it should tell the player the relevant gameplay truth for that surface. If the player is inspecting a combatant in battle, the text should reflect the current weapon, class skill, artifact, cooldown, ammo, and condition state that actually matters during that encounter.

13.4 Combat Resolution Order Reference
For technical reasoning, the encounter engine should be understood as operating on layered state snapshots rather than as one monolithic simultaneous event.
Battle construction establishes the active combatants, their current loadouts, starting positions, effective max HP, artifact access, and any mode-authored modifications.
At the start of each round, initiative order is computed once from effective Speed and the stated tiebreakers. That order is then fixed for the round.
Start-of-round effects resolve in initiative order.
Both sides then queue their main actions simultaneously.
Main actions resolve one at a time in initiative order. After each action resolves, the battlefield state may change immediately: HP changes, knockouts occur, frontline replacement can happen, conditions can be applied or refreshed, cooldown and ammo resources can change, and reactive effects can trigger if their conditions are met.
Because state is updated after each action, later actions resolve against the current live board state rather than the state that existed when plans were chosen.
After all main actions have resolved, the same pattern repeats for bonus actions.
End-of-round effects then resolve, followed by ordinary round-based countdowns and later-round Ultimate Meter autofill.

13.5 Action-Failure and Legality Model
Fabled is intentionally not a fully guaranteed-plan game. The system allows a selected action to fail at resolution if the original assumptions are no longer true.
Common reasons include:
the target was knocked out earlier in the round
the target changed position and is no longer legal for the chosen action
frontline replacement altered lane structure
the acting unit was knocked out or prevented from acting
a once-per-round or once-per-encounter reactive effect invalidated the attack window
This behavior is not merely an edge case. It is part of the skill expression of planning around initiative and disruption.

13.6 AI Planning Assumptions
The AI is intended to plan under the same legality rules as the player rather than receiving hidden exemptions.
AI planning should assume:
initiative order for both teams is knowable at plan time from current effective Speed
legal targets depend on current formation, lane rules, conditions, and weapon type
earlier actions can disrupt later actions by movement, knockout, immunity windows, or reactive effects
resource states such as ammo, cooldowns, attunement access, and once-per-encounter triggers matter
The AI may evaluate likely disruption and avoid fragile lines, but it still commits actions before full resolution just like the player does. It should not be documented or implemented as omniscient about outcomes that are not derivable from the current public state.
Quest AI, Bout AI, and tutorial enemy scripts may differ in selection policy, but they all hand actions to the same core encounter engine.

13.7 Save, Run, and Encounter State Objects
Fabled operates on three practical state layers.
Persistent profile state lives across sessions. This includes Gold, Reputation, Guild Exp, Guild Level-related progress, tutorial completion, employee selections, cosmetic ownership and equipment, friend records, favorite adventurer selections, and other account-wide flags.
Run state lives across encounters inside a Quest or Bout. This includes the current six-member party, current Artifact Pool, encounter score, win-loss record, streak state, and per-run economy values.
Encounter state lives only inside the current battle. This includes current HP, current position, queued actions, current conditions, cooldown counters, ammo, round counters, temporary markers, and Ultimate Meter values.

13.8 Representative Runtime State Fields
The live save model is expected to cover at least the following categories:
Campaign profile fields such as persistent Gold, Reputation, tutorial flags, gameplay settings, employee skill selections, cosmetic unlocks, and social data
Quest run fields such as active status, current wins and losses, streak state, opponent values, run gold, run reputation, active team snapshot, and Artifact Pool
Bout run fields such as mode, current score, opponent context, adaptation state, and temporary economic results
These objects are implementation details, but the game manual records them because they explain why some values reset between encounters while others persist through a run or across the whole account.

13.9 Rulebook Parsing Boundary
The body of this manual exists for human-readable technical reference. The appendices are additionally used as live structured data sources.
Appendix A is consumed for adventurer reference syncing.
Appendix C is consumed for standard artifact reference syncing.
Appendix D is consumed for enemy artifact reference syncing.
Because of that, prose may expand freely before Appendix A, but the appendix anchors and their entry structure must remain stable for runtime tools that parse them.

14. GLOSSARY
Action. The main selected move for a combatant during the Action phase.
Active Three. The currently deployed combatants in an encounter.
Artifact Pool. The unequipped artifacts available in the current run.
Attunement. The class restriction governing whether an equipped artifact's spell may be used.
Bench. The party members not deployed into the current encounter.
Bout. A best-of-three competitive set.
Encounter. A single battle between two active teams.
Formation. The placement of combatants into Frontline, Backline Left, and Backline Right.
Guild Exp. Persistent account progression experience.
Lane. One of the three targetable columns formed by the triangle formation.
Loadout. A combatant's chosen primary weapon, class, class skill, and artifact.
Locale. The regional source pool for a quest encounter's enemies.
Match Rating. The rating used to scale quest-enemy strength.
Primary Weapon. The active weapon used for Strike and active weapon-based passives.
Quest. The main PvE gauntlet mode that ends at three losses.
Reactive Spell. A spell that triggers automatically when its condition is satisfied.
Run. The current Quest, Bout, or Tutorial progression in progress.
Scout. The information-reveal step before selecting which three to deploy.
