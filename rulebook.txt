FABLED
A Guildmaster’s Game
Official Rulebook

You just inherited a guild in the kingdom of Fantasia, home of the mystical and magical. However, the guild has a rather poor reputation, and it’s up to you to turn your family’s fortunes around.
Luckily, adventurers with prestige too poor for other guilds still frequent your establishment, and you know how to get the best out of even the most motley crews. Form parties and send them on quests to build up your reputation and get some capital. It’s only a matter of time before you own the greatest guild in Fantasia!

1. OVERVIEW
Fabled is a tactical turn-based combat game focused on positioning, ability timing, and party optimization. The player manages a Guild Hall, assembles parties of adventurers, and sends them into encounters across two game modes: Quests and Bouts.
In Quests, the player faces a gauntlet of AI-controlled encounters, earning Gold and Reputation until three encounters are lost. In Bouts, the player competes in a best-of-three series against another player or AI, with both Random and Focused variants.
Between encounters, players adapt their strategy by adjusting loadouts, acquiring Artifacts, and recruiting new adventurers. Outside of encounters, the Guild Hall provides persistent progression through Guild Level, employee management, and the Market.
This rulebook is organized as follows: Section 2 defines the core components of the game. Section 3 details encounter rules. Sections 4 and 5 describe the two game modes. Sections 6 through 8 cover progression, the Guild Hall, and the Market. Appendices A through C contain reference data for all Adventurers, Class Skills, and Artifacts.

2. CORE CONCEPTS
2.1 Adventurers
Adventurers are the characters the player recruits and sends into encounters. Each adventurer is a unique individual with fixed innate attributes and configurable loadout attributes.
Innate Attributes
Every adventurer has the following attributes, which are fixed and cannot be changed:
Innate Skill. A passive ability unique to the adventurer.
Two Signature Weapons. The adventurer’s personal weapons. See 2.3 Weapons.
Ultimate Spell. A powerful ability fueled by the party’s Ultimate Meter. See 3.5 The Ultimate Meter.
Stats
Every adventurer has four stats:
HP. The adventurer’s maximum health. An adventurer is knocked out when their HP reaches 0.
Attack. Determines the damage dealt by the adventurer’s Strikes.
Defense. Reduces the damage the adventurer takes from abilities.
Speed. Determines the adventurer’s initiative order within a round. Higher Speed acts first.
Stat bonuses and penalties from multiple sources do not stack. When calculating stats, the game uses the single highest bonus and the single highest penalty currently applied to the adventurer.
Loadout Attributes
The following attributes are chosen by the player and can be changed between encounters:
Primary Weapon. One of the adventurer’s two Signature Weapons. The other becomes the secondary weapon.
Class. One of six roles. See 2.4 Classes.
Class Skill. A passive effect determined by the adventurer’s Class. See 2.4 Classes.
Artifact (optional). An equippable item drawn from the player’s Artifact pool. See 2.5 Artifacts.
2.2 Skills and Spells
Adventurers gain Skills and Spells from their innate attributes, weapons, Class Skills, and Artifacts.
Skills — passive effects that are always active while their source is active (e.g., while the relevant weapon is equipped as primary).
Spells — activated effects that consume an action to use. After a Spell is cast, it goes on cooldown for a specified number of rounds before it can be used again.
There is no limit to the number of Skills or Spells an adventurer can have simultaneously.
2.3 Weapons
Weapons determine the strength and type of an adventurer’s Strike action. Every weapon has a Power value and may provide additional Skills and Spells. Weapons come in three types:
Melee. Can only be used to Strike from the frontline. Can only target the enemy frontline.
Ranged. Can target the enemy frontline or a backline enemy in the user’s lane. Consumes Ammo with each Strike. Once Ammo is depleted, the weapon cannot be used to Strike.
Magic. Follows the same targeting rules as Ranged weapons. Striking with a Magic weapon counts as casting a Spell and triggers all relevant effects, and the weapon goes on cooldown after use.

Each adventurer has two Signature Weapons. Before an encounter, the player designates one as the primary weapon and the other as the secondary weapon. Only the primary weapon’s Skills and Spells are active during the encounter.
2.4 Classes
There are six classes. Each class defines a role and grants access to two Class Skills, of which the player selects one. Class Skills provide a passive effect and increase the adventurer’s maximum HP by a fixed amount.
Warden — Tanks and position manipulators.
Fighter — Melee damage dealers.
Mage — Magic damage dealers.
Ranger — Ranged damage dealers.
Cleric — Healers and enchanters.
Rogue — Disruptors and speedsters.

A party of six may contain at most one adventurer of each Class. See Appendix B for the full list of Class Skills.
2.5 Artifacts
Artifacts are equippable items that raise one of the holder’s stats and grant a Spell. Artifacts are managed through an Artifact pool, which is separate from the party itself. The pool holds Artifacts that are not currently equipped to any adventurer.

Artifact Rules
Only one copy of each Artifact may be equipped across the party at any time.
Each Artifact specifies one or more classes that can attune to it. An Artifact’s Spell can only be used if the holder’s Class matches one of the Artifact’s attunement classes.
An adventurer does not need to hold an Artifact. The Artifact slot is optional.
Some Artifacts have Reactive Spells. Reactive Spells are not actions; they trigger automatically when their condition is met, then go on cooldown. Triggering a Reactive Spell counts as casting a Spell.
Acquiring Artifacts
At the start of a Quest or Random Bout, three random Artifacts are added to the player’s Artifact pool. The three will not share any attunement classes, and every Class in the party will be able to attune to at least one of the three. Additional Artifacts can be earned as Quest encounter rewards. In Focused Bouts, the player chooses their own Artifacts. See Sections 4 and 5 for details.
See Appendix C for the full list of Artifacts.
2.6 Parties
A party consists of six adventurers. Only three are brought into any given encounter. The remaining three sit out but retain their loadouts and equipped Artifacts.
Parties are assembled at the start of a Quest or Bout. The method of assembly differs by mode (see Sections 4 and 5). A party may contain at most one adventurer of each Class and at most one copy of each Artifact equipped at a time.

3. ENCOUNTERS
An encounter is a single battle between two parties of three adventurers. Encounters are composed of rounds; each round, every adventurer acts at least once. This section describes the rules governing all encounters, regardless of game mode.
3.1 Formation
Each party uses a triangle formation consisting of one frontline position and two backline positions (left and right). The frontlines of both parties face each other, with their respective backlines behind them.

Player A Frontline
Player B Frontline
Player B Backline LeftPlayer B Backline Right

The three columns formed by these positions are called lanes. Each backline position shares a lane with the opposing backline position directly across from it. The frontline occupies a central lane shared by both frontline positions.
Formation Rules
Backline adventurers have their Speed halved.
Exactly one adventurer must occupy the frontline at all times. Up to two adventurers may occupy the backline.
If the frontline adventurer is knocked out, the leftmost backline adventurer automatically moves to the frontline.
3.2 Round Structure
Each round proceeds through the following phases in order:
Initiative Check. Each adventurer’s Speed (including backline penalty, stat bonuses, and stat penalties) is evaluated. Adventurers are ordered from highest to lowest Speed. Initiative is only checked once at the start of the round. In the case of a Speed tie, the adventurer with the lower Power primary weapon acts first. If that is also tied, it is decided by which adventurer’s party has the higher average Speed, or a coinflip if that returns equal.
Start of Round Effects. All “Start of Round” effects resolve in initiative order.
Action Selection. Each player simultaneously selects one action for each of their adventurers.
Action Resolution. Each adventurer’s selected action resolves in initiative order.
Bonus Action Selection. Each player selects one bonus action for each adventurer that has one available.
Bonus Action Resolution. Bonus actions resolve in initiative order.
End of Round Effects. All “End of Round” and “Each Round” effects resolve in initiative order.
3.3 Actions
During the Action Selection phase, an adventurer must choose exactly one of the following five actions:
Strike
The adventurer attacks a target with their primary weapon. Targeting rules depend on weapon type (see 2.3 Weapons). Damage is calculated as:

Damage = Power × (Attack / Defense), rounded up

The target’s HP is reduced by the damage amount. If the target’s HP reaches 0, they are knocked out and removed from the encounter.
Spread Strikes target all legal targets simultaneously but deal half damage to each.
Spellcast
The adventurer casts one of their available Spells (from weapons, Artifacts, or Class Skills). Spells that target enemies may target enemies in the caster’s lane or the enemy frontline. Spells that target allies may target any ally. After casting, the Spell goes on cooldown.
Switch Weapons
The adventurer swaps their primary and secondary weapons. All cooldowns on both weapons are reset. Ranged weapons fully reload their Ammo.
Swap Positions
The adventurer swaps positions with a target ally. If the target ally has not yet resolved their action this round, they resolve it from their new position. Each player may select the Swap action for at most one adventurer per round; however, other effects (such as abilities) may still cause additional swaps.
Skip
The adventurer takes no action.

3.4 Conditions
Conditions are temporary status effects applied to adventurers. Multiple different conditions can affect the same adventurer simultaneously, but the same condition does not stack with itself (a new application refreshes the duration).

Condition
Effect
Burn
The adventurer takes 8% of their max HP as damage at the end of each round.
Root
The adventurer cannot use the Swap Positions action.
Shock
The adventurer has −25 Speed and their Strikes deal 15% of the damage back to themselves as recoil.

The adventurer deals 15% less damage.

The adventurer takes 15% more damage.

The adventurer takes 15% less damage.

3.5 The Ultimate Meter
Each party has an Ultimate Meter that starts at 0. When an adventurer uses a non-Magic Strike, the meter increases by +1. When an adventurer casts a non-Ultimate Spell, the meter increases by +2. When the meter reaches 5, any adventurer in the party may use their Ultimate Spell as their action. Casting an Ultimate Spell depletes the meter to 0.
Starting from round 7, each player’s Ultimate Meter automatically fills by +1 at the end of each round. Starting from round 12, this auto-fill increases to +2 per round.
3.6 Ending an Encounter
An encounter ends immediately when either of the following conditions is met:
Party Elimination. All three of one party’s adventurers are knocked out. The other party wins.
Ultimate Victory. A party casts their third Ultimate Spell during the encounter. That party wins immediately.

4. QUESTS
Quests are the primary single-player game mode. The player faces a gauntlet of encounters against AI-controlled enemies, earning Reputation and Gold. A Quest ends when the player has accumulated three losses. Quest enemies are not adventurers—they are creatures, monsters, and rival forces from the world of Fantasia whose strength scales with the player’s Match Rating (see 4.8 Matchmaking).
4.1 Starting a Quest
To begin a Quest, the player completes the following steps in order:

Step 1: Choose a Favorite. The player selects one adventurer they have previously quested with. This adventurer is guaranteed a place in the party.
Step 2: Receive a Party. Five additional adventurers are randomly assigned to the party. The random selection is constrained so that, based on each adventurer’s preferred or optimal classes, at least two full sets of six distinct classes are achievable among the six party members.
Step 3: Receive Starting Artifacts. Three random Artifacts are added to the player’s Artifact pool. No two of the three share any attunement classes, and every Class represented in the party can attune to at least one of the three.
Step 4: Set Loadouts. The player assigns each adventurer a Primary Weapon, Class, and Class Skill, and may equip Artifacts from the pool. Only one of each Class may be assigned across the party. Only one copy of each Artifact may be equipped at a time. Unequipped Artifacts remain in the pool.
4.2 Encounter Flow
Each encounter within a Quest proceeds through these steps:

Step 1: Scout. The player sees the enemy party of three.
Step 2: Select and Form. The player selects three adventurers from their party and assigns starting positions. During this formation phase, the player may swap Artifacts between any party member (including unequipping from the three sitting out) and change any selected adventurer’s Class Skill. The player may not equip Artifacts from the pool or change any adventurer’s Class.
Step 3: Fight. The encounter resolves under the rules in Section 3.
Step 4: Results. The player receives or loses Reputation and collects rewards or takes penalties. See 4.3 and 4.4.
Step 5: Regroup. The player may alter their entire party’s loadouts: Primary Weapon, Class, Class Skill, and Artifact assignments from the full Artifact pool. This is the only phase where the pool is freely accessible.
4.3 Winning a Quest Encounter
When the player wins an encounter, two things happen: Reputation is gained and the player chooses one reward.
Reputation Gain
Reputation is added to the Quest’s running gain total using the following formula:

ReputationGain = round(BaseChange + WinstreakBonus), clamped from 8 to 30

BaseChange = 28 × (ActualScore − ExpectedScore)
ActualScore = 1 (always 1 on a win).
ExpectedScore = 1 / (1 + 10^((OpponentReputation − PlayerReputation) / 400))
WinstreakBonus = min(current Quest winstreak before this win, 5) × 2
Encounter Reward
After gaining Reputation, the player selects one of three reward options:

Option A — Gold. Gain 100 Gold, added to the Quest’s unspent Gold pool.

Option B — Artifact. Choose one Artifact from a selection of three. The three offered Artifacts will each grant a different stat bonus, and where possible, no two will share the same attunement classes. The chosen Artifact is added to the Artifact pool and may be equipped between encounters. Standard Artifact rules apply.

Option C — Recruit (costs 100 Gold). Spend 100 Gold from the Quest’s unspent Gold pool to replace one adventurer in the party with a new one. The player is shown three randomly selected adventurers, each of a markedly different role or preferred class. The player chooses one; the replaced adventurer leaves, and any Artifact they held returns to the pool. This option may be selected multiple times in a single reward phase, each costing an additional 100 Gold with a fresh selection of three candidates. The new adventurer’s loadout must be set before the next encounter.
4.4 Losing a Quest Encounter
When the player loses an encounter:
−10 Reputation is subtracted from the Quest’s running gain total.
−50 Gold is subtracted from the Quest’s unspent Gold pool (the pool cannot drop below 0).

The player’s loss count increases by one. When the loss count reaches three, the Quest ends.
4.5 End of Quest
When the Quest concludes (after the third loss or by forfeit), the following steps are resolved in order:

1. Sell Artifacts. All Artifacts in the player’s possession—equipped and in the pool—are sold for 100 Gold each. The Gold is added to the Quest’s Gold pool.
2. Collect Gold. The Quest’s total unspent Gold (including Artifact sale proceeds) is added to the player’s permanent Gold balance. This amount may be modified by the Assistant’s active skill (see 7.2 The Assistant).
3. Apply Reputation. The Quest’s running Reputation gain total is added to the player’s permanent Reputation. This value may be positive or negative.
4. Earn Exp. The player earns Guild Exp. See 6.1 Earning Experience.

Successful Quest: A Quest is considered successful if the final net Reputation gain total is positive. This distinction affects certain Guild Hall employee skills (see Section 7).
4.6 Forfeiting a Quest
The player may forfeit a Quest at any time. Forfeiting ends the Quest immediately and applies a penalty:

Forfeit Penalty = −10 Reputation × losses remaining

Losses remaining equals 3 minus the player’s current loss count. If the player has 1 loss and forfeits, they had 2 remaining, so the penalty is −20 Reputation applied to the Quest’s gain total. After the penalty, the standard End of Quest steps proceed.

4.8 Matchmaking
Quest enemy strength scales with the player’s Match Rating:

Match Rating = Reputation + (20 × current winstreak) − (15 × current lossstreak)

The winstreak modifier pushes successful players toward tougher enemies. The lossstreak modifier provides slightly easier encounters after consecutive losses. Enemy parties always consist of three enemies.

5. BOUTS
Bouts are Fabled’s competitive encounter mode. A Bout is a best-of-three series: the first player to win two encounters wins the Bout. There are two types: Random Bouts and Focused Bouts.
Bouts may be played against another player over LAN (with server-based online play planned for the future) or against an AI opponent. Unlike Quest enemies, enemy parties in Bouts are always composed of adventurers—the same heroes available to the player.
5.1 Random Bouts
In a Random Bout, each player receives a randomly assembled party and Artifact pool, mirroring the Quest setup.
Setup
Step 1: Receive a Party. Each player selects a favorite adventurer, then receives five random adventurers. The same class-diversity constraint from Quests applies.
Step 2: Receive Starting Artifacts. Each player receives three random Artifacts in their pool, following the same attunement diversity rules as Quests.
Step 3: Set Loadouts. Each player sets their party’s loadouts, including equipping Artifacts from the pool.
Encounter Flow
Each encounter follows these steps:

Step 1: Scout. Both players see each other’s full party of six.
Step 2: Select and Form. Each player selects three adventurers and assigns positions. During formation, players may swap Artifacts between party members and change Class Skills. Players may not access the Artifact pool or change Classes.
Step 3: Fight. The encounter resolves under the rules in Section 3.
Step 4: Adapt. After the encounter (if the Bout is not yet decided), each player makes one free adaptation:
Add an Artifact: Choose one Artifact from a selection of three randomly offered (different stat bonuses, no shared attunements where possible) and add it to the pool.
Replace an Adventurer: Swap one party member for a new one chosen from three randomly offered candidates of different roles. The replaced adventurer leaves; any Artifact they held returns to the pool.

Players may also freely adjust all loadouts (Primary Weapon, Class, Class Skill, and Artifact assignments from the pool) before the next encounter.

Entry Fee and Rewards
Against another player: the entry fee and payout are determined by the Bartender’s active skill (see 7.3 The Bartender). Each player pays their own Bartender’s fee and receives their own Bartender’s payout on a win. The losing player forfeits their entry fee and receives nothing. Random Bouts do not affect Reputation or Rank.
Against AI: Random Bouts against AI have no entry fee and no Gold reward. They still award Guild Exp.
5.2 Focused Bouts
In a Focused Bout, each player builds their own party from scratch using the full adventurer roster and Artifact list. Focused Bouts have no entry fee and no rewards—they exist purely as a competitive and practice format.
Setup
Step 1: Build a Party. Each player constructs a party of six adventurers from the full roster. The standard constraint applies: at most one of each Class.
Step 2: Choose Artifacts. Each player equips Artifacts to their adventurers and places up to three additional Artifacts in their Artifact pool. All Artifacts are chosen from the full list. Standard rules apply.
Step 3: Set Loadouts. Each player assigns all loadout attributes.
Encounter Flow
Each encounter follows these steps:

Step 1: Scout. Both players see each other’s full party of six.
Step 2: Select and Form. Each player selects three and assigns positions. During formation, players may swap Artifacts between party members and change Class Skills. Players may not access the pool or change Classes.
Step 3: Fight. The encounter resolves under the rules in Section 3.
Step 4: Adjust. After the encounter (if the Bout is not yet decided), each player may change loadouts using only the existing roster and pool. This includes reassigning Primary Weapons, Classes, Class Skills, and moving Artifacts between adventurers and the pool. No new adventurers or Artifacts are added.
Rewards
Focused Bouts award Guild Exp (see 6.1) but no Gold, Reputation, or Rank changes.

6.3 Level-Up Rewards
Gold
On every level-up, the player receives Gold:

Level-Up Gold = 50 + (10 × new level)

Level 2 grants 70 Gold. Level 10 grants 150 Gold. Level 25 grants 300 Gold. This amount may be modified by the Assistant’s active skill (see 7.2).
Cosmetics
At certain milestones, the player receives cosmetic items:
Every 5 levels (5, 10, 15, 20, …): one random Common cosmetic. Common cosmetics have a Gold value of 650.
Every 10 levels (10, 20, 30, 40, …): one random Rare cosmetic, in addition to the Common cosmetic. Rare cosmetics have a Gold value of 2,000.

Duplicate Protection: If the player already owns all cosmetics of the awarded rarity, the item is automatically sold and the player receives its Gold value instead (650 for Common, 2,000 for Rare).

7. THE GUILD HALL
The Guild Hall is the player’s home base. It serves as the hub for party management, Quest and Bout preparation, Market access, and employee management. The Guild Hall is staffed by three employees, each of whom provides a passive economic benefit.
Each employee has one or more skills. The player selects one active skill per employee. Skills may be changed at any time between Quests and Bouts but are locked for the duration of an active Quest or Bout. Skills are designed around committed playstyles: each offers a distinct advantage with a corresponding tradeoff to discourage frequent swapping.
7.1 Employees
The three employees are:
The Assistant — affects non-Reputation rewards from Quests and Guild leveling. Five skills.
The Bartender — sets the entry fee and payout for Random Bouts against other players. Five skills.
The Server — provides a daily bonus tied to a favorite adventurer. One skill (always active).

Employee appearances can be customized with skins purchased from the Market (see Section 8).
7.2 The Assistant
The Assistant manages the guild’s finances. Their active skill modifies non-Reputation rewards from Quests and from leveling the guild. Reputation is never altered by any Assistant skill.

Shrewd
The player receives 25% more Gold when collecting unspent Gold at the end of a Quest. If the Quest was successful, the bonus increases to 50%.
Tradeoff: No artifact sale bonus, no Exp conversion, no level-up bonus. Pure Gold-per-Quest optimization.
Resourceful
The player receives 50% more Gold when selling Artifacts at the end of a Quest and when receiving Gold from duplicate cosmetics as level-up rewards.
Tradeoff: No bonus to unspent Gold collection. Rewards artifact-hoarding Quest strategies.

Erudite
All Gold the player would gain from a Quest (unspent Gold and Artifact sales combined) is immediately converted to Guild Exp at a rate of 1 Exp per Gold. The player receives zero Gold from the Quest.
Tradeoff: Complete loss of Quest income. The player must rely on Bouts and level-up rewards for Gold.
Appraiser
Artifacts sell for 150 Gold instead of 100 Gold at the end of a Quest. However, the Recruit reward option costs 150 Gold instead of 100 Gold.
Tradeoff: More valuable artifact sales, but recruiting mid-Quest becomes significantly more expensive.
Ambitious
Level-up Gold rewards are doubled. However, unspent Gold collected from Quests is reduced by 25%.
Tradeoff: Stronger milestone paydays at the cost of consistent Quest income. Best at higher levels where level-up Gold is large.
7.3 The Bartender
The Bartender runs the guild’s Bout operations. Their active skill determines the entry fee and payout for Random Bouts played against other players. Random Bouts against AI and all Focused Bouts are unaffected by the Bartender.

Safe Income
Entry fee: 100 Gold. Payout on win: 200 Gold.
Net: +100 on win, −100 on loss. Low risk, steady income.
High Roller
The player chooses an entry fee between 300 and 600 Gold. Payout on win: 2× the entry fee. If the Bout is won 2–0, the payout is 3× the entry fee instead.
Net at 600: +600 on 2–1, +1,200 on 2–0, −600 on loss. Maximum upside for confident players.
Generous Pour
Entry fee: 200 Gold. Payout on win: 500 Gold.
Net: +300 on win, −200 on loss. Strong payout with moderate risk.

Penny Pincher
Entry fee: 0 Gold. Payout on win: 100 Gold.
Net: +100 on win, 0 on loss. Zero downside, capped upside.
Double or Nothing
Entry fee: 300 Gold. Payout on 2–1 win: 0 Gold (entry fee is lost). Payout on 2–0 win: 900 Gold.
Net: +600 on 2–0, −300 on 2–1, −300 on loss. Only profitable with consistent sweeps.
7.4 The Server
The Server has a single skill that is always active.

Friendly
Each day, the Server selects a favorite adventurer from the full roster. The favorite is displayed in the Guild Hall and changes daily. When the player wins a Bout or completes a successful Quest with the Server’s current favorite in their party of six (the adventurer does not need to be one of the three brought into an encounter), the Server awards the player 100 Gold.

8. THE MARKET
The Market is the in-game shop where Gold is spent on cosmetic items. All Market items are purely cosmetic and do not affect gameplay. Items unlock progressively as Guild Level increases; higher-level items are more expensive and visually elaborate.
For the purposes of level-up cosmetic rewards, Common items are those costing 650 Gold or less. Rare items cost more than 650 Gold.
